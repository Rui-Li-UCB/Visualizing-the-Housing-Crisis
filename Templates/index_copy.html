Everyone deserves housing.

And yet historical practices of redlining, zoning policy, evictions, displacement, rising rents, and lack of affordable housing are just some of the elements that contribute to the “housing crisis.”

Welcome to Richmond, CA

Richmond presents a compelling case study to understand the housing crisis.

Located in the East San Francisco Bay Area, the city has been racially and economically diverse since its founding. Like many cities, Richmond has its own fault lines. There’s a history of housing inequity dating back to pre-WWII and a large corporate industry presence, both of which have contributed to early stages of gentrification and displacement of the city’s Black population.

Though Richmond has a unique history, the housing problems faced by there echo across many major American cities. Therefore, the story we set out to tell will be set in Richmond, but we hope learning about what has led to the housing crisis in a specific context can help with understanding this issue in its entirety.



Living in Richmond is Not the Same for Everyone

Today in Richmond, life looks different based simply on where people live. By examining disparities between White and Black populations of the city stemming from housing-related factors-including zoning, evictions, and home ownership-you will see these critical disparities clearly.

These disparities all equal the housing crisis, and by learning about them, their history, and effects, you are undoing the system as we strive for something better.

How Did We Get Here?

Zoning

Zoning laws define land use into categories such as commercial, residential, or mixed (commercial and residential) use. Often highly prescriptive, they also specify which kinds of homes can be built and where - for example, single-family homes with a certain setback as opposed to dense, multi-family apartment complexes.

This is the City of Richmond’s current zoning map.

This is the racial composition of Richmond in 2021.

As seen in South Richmond, the areas where the city placed temporary war housing projects - which housed the majority of shipyard workers, who were Black - still remain Black neighborhoods. Beyond Richmond, zoning ordinances have been a means of exclusion; it encodes in geographic reality the concentration of people of color into certain housing types, and parts of the city.

Much of the racial disparity in housing is due to the historical legacy and practice of redlining in the Bay Area.

Redlining was the discriminatory practice by banks and government agencies of denying or withholding home loans in “red” (“hazardous” ) neighborhoods in terms of “mortgage security”. This process excluded racial minorities, especially Black residents, from accessing mortgage opportunities in “green” (“safe investment”) and thus excluding them from living in such neighborhoods and concentrating them into neighborhoods with lower investment. This is an example of a redlining map for nearby Oakland, Berkeley, and other cities.

Zoning, then, is often correlated with redlined and segregated neighborhoods. Studies from the Othering and Belonging Institute show that single-family zoning is strongly correlated with neighborhood characteristics that have a direct impact on residents.

Evictions

When someone is legally evicted, they are ordered by the court to leave their residence. Using data from the Eviction Research Network on writs of restitution (when a tenant is officially removed from their home by the county sheriff after losing their eviction case) served from January 2017 to March 2022, we find that there are racial disparities in evictions in Richmond. As with zoning, who is evicted is related to one's socioeconomic and geographic reality.

Evictions

Being evicted from one's home has significant negative public health impacts.

Past research shows that eviction is linked with worse physical and mental health, more emergency room visits, lower educational outcomes for children, food insecurity, and higher rates of future homelessness.

All of these factors are tied with financial instability, which experiencing an eviction exacerbates. This is why federal, state, and local governments instituted moratoria on eviction in the early days of the COVID-19 pandemic.

Why are there still issues in housing? Problems surrounding development today

Developing housing is prohibitively costly and time consuming.

Multifamily developments must adhere to a variety of regulations in order to protect the health and safety of residents, and ensure the integrity of the building and surrounding area. While certain regulations are very important, it is important to consider that each regulation comes at an expense to the project and developer, who then in turn increase rents in order to maintain financial feasibility. At present, regulation compliance constitutes an average of 40% of a project's total development costs, a high percentage considering the cost of land, labor, and materials. Conflicting standards across regulations and time consuming review processes are costly both in terms of time and money. This in turn can limit who can afford to develop housing. Increasingly, development is being conducted by large corporations who have greater access to funding to be able to afford the cost of holding onto land through delays, rather than by smaller scale developers who may want to invest in the community.

Regulatory mandates have been seen as one potential solution to the creation of affordable housing. However, adhering to these regulations are also costly and may increase the total development costs by 30%, which can discourage housing development in the places that need it most.

Recently, the National Association of Home Builders (NAHB) and the National Multifamily Housing Council (NMHC) undertook a joint research effort to find out how much government regulation affects multifamily housing via a survey distributed to multifamily developers. It found that 87.5% of multifamily developers said they avoid building in jurisdictions with rent control in place.

Another obstacle to housing development is community engagement, which occurs during multiple stages of the review processes. While it is important for the community to contribute to the development of their surroundings, research has shown that “Not in My Backyard” (NIMBY) oppositions adds an average of 5.6% to total development costs, and can delay project development by an average of 7.4 months. Additionally, public facing projects tend to receive a lot of expectations to fulfill extraordinary “catch-all” solutions to a community's multi-faceted efforts.

There are no quick fixes to the housing crisis affecting residents in Richmond and beyond.
