Visualizing the Housing Crisis

in Richmond, CA

Everyone deserves housing.

And yet current & historical practices of exclusionary policy, rising rents, and slow development have contributed to the affordable housing shortage, inequitable evictions, and broad displacement that make up the “housing crisis” today. Whether or not you’ve heard of this term, it's important to understand the elements that make this such a complex problem.

We believe that knowledge can empower positive change— this crisis is complex, but this is a great place to get started.

Understanding the legacy of the past will help contextualize the present, and work towards a more just future—we hope you'll come away with an understand the affordable housing crisis by exploring this case study of Richmond, CA.


Welcome to Richmond, CA

Located in Contra Costa county in the East San Francisco Bay Area, the city of Richmond is racially and economically diverse. Richmond feels like the average American city. A small, working-class suburban community, it went through a period of post-WWII industrialization and has a vibrant town known for grassroots engagement, including in the housing space.

Living in Richmond is Not the Same for Everyone

Like many cities across the United States, life in Richmond is different depending on who you are and where you live. We see this most clearly by looking at the different impacts of the housing crisis on different groups of people, and most starkly in the housing outcomes between White and Black populations. Let’s look at some of these differences across evictions, zoning, and renting vs. homeownership.


Where you live

Zoning laws shape how we live. From commercial shopping centers to farms to new housing, they govern what can be built, and where. These highly prescriptive rules dictate construction between single-family homes and dense multi-family apartment complexes. Research by the Othering and Belonging Institute shows that residing in single-family zones is highly correlated with better quality-of-life: in fact, they state, “...cities with high levels of single-family zoning have greater resources in virtually every statistic we are able to measure.”

Placeholder for SFZ vs MFZ viz

Unfortunately, residence in single family zones break sharply on racial lines. White residents are more likely to live in single-family zones, while Black residents are more likely to reside in multifamily zones.

Who owns homes in Richmond?

Homeownership is one of the primary methods for building generational wealth and accessing the improved quality-of-life that comes with it. Owning a home can provide greater stability than renting by allowing residents to stay in their community, build long-term equity, and to invest in their community’s education and other public services through their home’s property taxes.  Like many other cities in California, Richmond’s homeownership has often been restricted to wealthy white neighborhoods and maintained through policies such as Proposition 13, which defines state increase in property tax. 

In Richmond, white residents own homes at more than 2x Black residents and 1.3x Hispanic residents. For renting, Hispanic residents rent almost 2x more and Black residents rent around 1.5x more than White residents.

Who faces eviction?

Who is evicted is related to one’s socioeconomic and geographic reality. Eviction is one of the most common methods of displacement, which is when a resident is forced to move against their will. Displacement can have negative effects ranging from worse physical and mental health, more emergency room visits, lower educational outcomes for children, food insecurity, to higher rates of future homelessness. Using data from when tenants are officially removed from their home by the county sheriff from January 2017 to March 2022, the Eviction Research Network finds that there are significant racial disparities in evictions in Richmond. Note that this dataset is a severe undercount of the actual number of evictions occurring, but it is still representative of a larger trend.

Using data on writs of restitution (when a tenant is officially removed from their home by the county sheriff after losing an eviction case) served from January 2017 to March 2022, the Eviction Research Network finds that there are significant racial disparities in evictions in Richmond. Note that this dataset is a severe undercount of the actual number of evictions occurring, but it is still representative of a larger trend.

Who is evicted is related to one’s socioeconomic and geographic reality. All of these factors are tied with financial instability and housing vulnerability, which experiencing an eviction exacerbates.

As seen in this map, the neighborhoods with the highest levels of recorded evictions are the Iron Triangle, Panhandle, and Hilltop. These neighborhoods are predominantly Black and Hispanic. The current housing landscape places the vulnerable - who are often racial minorities - in precarious situations rather than support them.

While moratoria reduced evictions overall during the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020, Black and Hispanic residents are still evicted at higher rates compared to White and Asian residents. In 2017, there were 3.5 Black and 2.5 Hispanic evictions for every 1 White eviction. In 2021, there were 3.5 Black evictions and 4 Hispanic eviction for every White eviction.

How did we get here?

These disparate outcomes for Black and White populations did not occur by accident: Much of the racial disparity in housing is due to the historical legacy of exclusionary policies, redlining, and segregation.

How Did We Get Here? Let's Go Back in Time

Now that we understand some of the issues, it is important to ask: how did Richmond become what it looks like today?  There is a clear history of segregation and racist policies that are rooted in the history of Richmond, informing the present housing reality. Richard Rothstein documents this history in his book, The Color of Law.

Rothstein’s novel tells the story of Richmond’s residents who moved to Richmond for work and saw the city’s population boom. Prior to World War 2, Richmond was a small, predominantly agricultural area.

However, as the shipyards ran out of White workers, Black workers from the South moved to Richmond in large numbers. However, segregated public housing and other racially based practices impacted where these residents could live. 

After the war, the population boom slowed as the government tore down its housing projects. Richmond then became an oil town, still with racial disparities.

Issues in Housing Continue Today

You might be thinking, how are these problems still prevalent today? Why aren't they being dealt with? Why don't we just build more housing?

Well, there are many reasons and perspectives in potential solutions.

Let's start with the perspectives from developers.

Problems surrounding development today

Multifamily developments must adhere to a variety of regulations in order to protect the health and safety of residents, and ensure the integrity of the building and surrounding area. While certain regulations are very important, it is important to consider that each regulation comes at an expense to the project and developer, who then in turn increase rents in order to maintain financial feasibility. At present, regulation compliance constitutes an average of 40% of a project's total development costs, a high percentage considering the cost of land, labor, and materials. Conflicting standards across regulations and time consuming review processes are costly both in terms of time and money. This in turn can limit who can afford to develop housing. Increasingly, development is being conducted by large corporations who have greater access to funding to be able to afford the cost of holding onto land through delays, rather than by smaller scale developers who may want to invest in the community.

What's the Fix?

There are no quick fixes to the housing crisis affecting residents in Richmond and beyond. We've summarized several approaches being pursued by people across policy, advocacy, and grassroots/community. However, we definitely need movement across all of these areas and more. Some of these are contradictory solutions.
