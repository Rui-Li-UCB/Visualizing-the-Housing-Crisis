Visualizing the Housing Crisis

in Richmond, CA

Everyone deserves housing.

And yet historical practices of redlining, zoning policy, evictions, displacement, rising rents, and lack of affordable housing are just some of the elements that contribute to the “housing crisis.”

Whether or not you've heard of this term, you might be aware of some of these elements that make this such a complex problem. The legacy of segregation-based practices and policy contributes to the continuation of racial inequity in housing.

Understanding the past will help us contextualize the present and work towards a better future— by learning more about this crisis, we hope to encourage more nuanced discussions.

Welcome to Richmond, CA

Richmond presents a compelling case study to understand the housing crisis.

Located in the East San Francisco Bay Area, the city has been racially and economically diverse since its founding. Like many cities, Richmond has its own fault lines. There’s a history of housing inequity dating back to pre-WWII and a large corporate industry presence, both of which have contributed to early stages of gentrification and displacement of the city’s Black population.

Though Richmond has a unique history, the housing problems faced by there echo across many major American cities. Therefore, the story we set out to tell will be set in Richmond, but we hope learning about what has led to the housing crisis in a specific context can help with understanding this issue in its entirety.

Living in Richmond is Not the Same for Everyone

Today in Richmond, life looks different based simply on where people live. By examining disparities between White and Black populations of the city stemming from housing-related factors-including zoning, evictions, and home ownership-you will see these critical disparities clearly.

These disparities all equal the housing crisis, and by learning about them, their history, and effects, you are undoing the system as we strive for something better.


Housing Today: Different Realities

Zoning laws define land use into categories such as commercial, residential, or mixed (commercial and residential) use. Often highly prescriptive, they specify which kinds of homes can be built and where - for example, single-family homes with a certain setback as opposed to dense, multi-family apartment complexes. 

Research by the Othering and Belonging Institute finds that residents of single-family zones have access to better schools, cleaner water, lower exposure to pollutants, higher incomes, lower property, and higher property values. In fact, they state, “...cities with high levels of single-family zoning have greater resources in virtually every statistic we are able to measure.”

Renting vs. Owning: Building Generational Wealth

Understanding homeownership versus renting is important because it provides insight into who has access to the benefits that come with generational wealth. Owning a home can provide greater stability than renting in respect to staying in a community, and property taxes from homes provide funding for better education and other public services. In California as a whole, homeownership has often been restricted to wealthy white neighborhoods and maintained through policies such as Proposition 13, which defines changes in property tax.

In terms of owning, White residents own homes at more than 2x Black residents and 1.3x Hispanic residents. For renting, Hispanic residents rent almost 2x more and Black residents rent around 1.5x more than White residents.

Evictions

In a legal eviction, someone is ordered by a court to leave their residence. Eviction is one of the most common methods of displacement, which is when a resident is forced to move against their will. Displacement can have negative effects ranging from worse physical and mental health, more emergency room visits, lower educational outcomes for children, food insecurity, to higher rates of future homelessness.

Using data on writs of restitution (when a tenant is officially removed from their home by the county sheriff after losing an eviction case) served from January 2017 to March 2022, the Eviction Research Network finds that there are significant racial disparities in evictions in Richmond. Note that this dataset is a severe undercount of the actual number of evictions occurring, but it is still representative of a larger trend.

Who is evicted is related to one’s socioeconomic and geographic reality. All of these factors are tied with financial instability and housing vulnerability, which experiencing an eviction exacerbates.

As seen in this map, the neighborhoods with the highest levels of recorded evictions are the Iron Triangle, Panhandle, and Hilltop. These neighborhoods are predominantly Black and Hispanic. The current housing landscape places the vulnerable - who are often racial minorities - in precarious situations rather than support them.

While moratoria reduced evictions overall during the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020, Black and Hispanic residents are still evicted at higher rates compared to White and Asian residents. In 2017, there were 3.5 Black and 2.5 Hispanic evictions for every 1 White eviction. In 2021, there were 3.5 Black evictions and 4 Hispanic eviction for every White eviction.

Much of the racial disparity in housing is due to the historical legacy and practice of redlining in the Bay Area.

Redlining was the discriminatory practice by banks and government agencies of denying or withholding home loans in “red” (“hazardous” ) neighborhoods in terms of “mortgage security”. This process excluded racial minorities, especially Black residents, by limiting their access to home loans. This process segregated cities by concentrating Black families into neighborhoods with lower financial investment, separated from White neighborhoods.

...in each city, assigned grades to residential neighborhoods that reflected their "mortgage security" that would then be visualized on color-coded maps. Neighborhoods receiving the highest grade of "A"—colored green on the maps—were deemed minimal risks for banks and other mortgage lenders when they were determining who should received loans and which areas in the city were safe investments. Those receiving the lowest grade of "D," colored red, were considered "hazardous."

How Did We Get Here? Let's Go Back in Time

Now that we understand some of the issues, it is important to ask: how did Richmond become what it looks like today?  There is a clear history of segregation and racist policies that are rooted in the history of Richmond, informing the present housing reality. Richard Rothstein documents this history in his book, The Color of Law.

Rothstein’s novel tells the story of Richmond’s residents who moved to Richmond for work and saw the city’s population boom. Prior to World War 2, Richmond was a small, predominantly agricultural area.

However, as the shipyards ran out of White workers, Black workers from the South moved to Richmond in large numbers. However, segregated public housing and other racially based practices impacted where these residents could live. 

After the war, the population boom slowed as the government tore down its housing projects. Richmond then became an oil town, still with racial disparities.

Issues in Housing Continue Today

You might be thinking, how are these problems still prevalent today? Why aren't they being dealt with? Why don't we just build more housing?

Well, there are many reasons and perspectives in potential solutions.

Let's start with the perspectives from developers.

Problems surrounding development today

Multifamily developments must adhere to a variety of regulations in order to protect the health and safety of residents, and ensure the integrity of the building and surrounding area. While certain regulations are very important, it is important to consider that each regulation comes at an expense to the project and developer, who then in turn increase rents in order to maintain financial feasibility. At present, regulation compliance constitutes an average of 40% of a project's total development costs, a high percentage considering the cost of land, labor, and materials. Conflicting standards across regulations and time consuming review processes are costly both in terms of time and money. This in turn can limit who can afford to develop housing. Increasingly, development is being conducted by large corporations who have greater access to funding to be able to afford the cost of holding onto land through delays, rather than by smaller scale developers who may want to invest in the community.

What's the Fix?

There are no quick fixes to the housing crisis affecting residents in Richmond and beyond. We've summarized several approaches being pursued by people across policy, advocacy, and grassroots/community. However, we definitely need movement across all of these areas and more. Some of these are contradictory solutions.
